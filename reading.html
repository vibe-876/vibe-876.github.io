Reading

Reading is one of the great joys of my life. Some of my favorites are shown below.

I am currently reading The Cathedral and the Bazaar, by Eric S. Raymond.

Authors That I Recommend

  • Kazuo Ishiguro

    • Kazuo Ishiguro is one of, if not my favorite author. I would highly recommend Never let me Go, and if you like that, then Klara and the Sun. I have a copy of The Buried Giant sat on my desk, so I'll probably read that at some point.

    Never Let Me Go is one of the few books that I can say changed the way that I looked at the world, with Klara and The Sun being another.

    Seriously, Never Let Me Go might be the best book I have ever read. Go and read it, and you'll see what I mean.

  • JRR Tolkien

    • If you haven't yet read any Tolkien, give it a shot. There's a good reason why he's considered one of the best writters to ever live.

  • HP Lovecraft

    • I've always had a thing for horror. I got given a copy of the compilation of all of HP Lovecraft's stories. I'm about a third of the way through so far, as I often read it in chunks between other books.

    Lovecraft has an interesting way of writing. Although some parts may seem a bit cliche by todays standards (the ending of Dagon comes to mind), there can be beautiful world building at other times (such as in The Doom That Came to Saranarth, The Transition of Juan Romero, and The White Ship).

    That being said, I don't think that Lovecraft is for everyone; it can be a little strange at times, and that's not everybodies cup of tea. But hey, you'll never know until you try.

Fiction Books That I Recommend

  • A Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes

    • I was quite young when I first read the Hunger Games, and I haven't really re-read them since, but I remember them fondly, and do want to re-read them some time. And A Ballad of Songbirds and Snakes has helped me come to that conclusion. I really liked how it showed the Capital's civilian's side on the war, and how it wasn't only the districts that had it bad. Lucy Gray and Coriolanus are two very memorable characters, and I feel that the ending did some justice for both of them.

  • Nineteen Eighty Four

    • Although I do feel that Orwell is a bit overrated, Nineteen Eighty Four definatly shows how why people love his books. You really do get some hope for Winston and Julia, and start to believe that even though Big Brother is all-knowing, they might still find a way out, which just makes it feel so much worse when they're mentally crushed at the end.

    I would say that this works as a good comparason to A Balled of Songbirds and Snakes: one works from the point of view of a higher-up in society, one from the working class; one shows somebody getting extreme punishment for their actions, one shows somebody getting rewarded.

Non-Fiction Books That I Recommend

  • The Cathedral and the Bazaar

    • The Cathedral and the Bazaar is a book by Eric S. Raymond that discusses the merits of open source software. It also starts (and ends) with the history of computer hackers, and how their society functions.

    I feel like the Cathedral and the Bazaar acts as a good partener to Free Software Free Society; the former discusses the tangable benifits to open source software, while the latter discusses the ethics of it. These two cover most of what any enthusiest would want to know.

  • C For Dummies

    • I never actually finished this book, as my bag thown into a puddle and it got soaked, but what I read was really good, and gave me a good foundation in C that I have since built from. C was not my first programming language (that title goes to Python), but it was the first that I chose to learn outside of school, and so this book has a bit of a special place in my heart.

    C For Dummies works as a good introduction to many concepts, and I would recommend it as a introductory book to programming in general.

  • Free Software Free Society

    • Stallman just has a way with words. This book brought 16 year old me to apprieciate free software, and the good that it can do for society. While it is not a book on programming, I would recommend it to any serious programmer, as if nothing else, ethics is a fun topic to learn about.

  • The Art of Functional Programming

    • I got a copy of The Art of Functional Programming when I was learning Haskell. It gives a good grounding in how functional methods work, and does a good job on concepts such as currying and abstracting functions, although I feel it was lacking in data types and the power of higher-order functions. I would still recommend this book to anybody who wants to learn about functional programming in Haskell or OCaml (Even though it uses OCaml, I did all of the exercises in Haskell instead, and learned a lot doing it).

  • Logic Programming with Prolog

    • This book worked as my first (and currently only) step into logical programming, and I can say that I am very happy that I read it. It does a good job of explaining concepts, and all of the exercises felt like they had a purpose. While I'll admit I don't have any other LP books that I can compare it to, I feel that Logic Programming with Prolog did a good job of teaching me the beginings.
